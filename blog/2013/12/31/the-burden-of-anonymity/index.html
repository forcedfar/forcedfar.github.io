The Burden of Anonymity

Dec 31st, 2013

I am one of approximately 7.13 billion people on this planet. Ideally, an adversary would have a one in 7.13 billion chance of connecting Forced Far to my real life identity. However, it is trivial to narrow this down and assume that I am a member of a much smaller subset of the world’s population. I am an English-speaking internet user. I know how to use Tor. There are currently approximately three million Tor users. According to some rough estimates and quick back-of-the-envelope math, less than a million Tor users live in English-speaking countries. Even taking every privacy precaution, my blog leaks enough information about me to eliminate over 99.986% of the population. A keen reader will observe that in making those calculations I essentially outed myself as one who lives in an English-speaking country. Leaks upon leaks.

Maintaining my anonymity is already exhausting. Every communication with every account related to Forced Far must take place via Tor. I can’t even view my blog without obscuring my IP address because in the early stages I would be the only visitor and therefore likely the originator. When logged in to Twitter, I have to be careful that I don’t view tweets or display behaviors that could leak more information. When doing research, I have to do all my searches through Tor. Every website I view and action I take online is potentially stored forever and could be used against me. It’s like Benjamin Franklin said: “Three men can keep a secret if two of them are dead and none of them received national security letters or used backdoored machines.”

This might seem overkill, but the goal is to protect my identity against even the most powerful of adversaries. If my anonymity would be compromised in the event that Github and my ISP are both hacked or forced to give up information under legal duress, then I have no anonymity. This is surprisingly stressful. Although I have taken significant precautions, I can’t be sure that I haven’t accidentally left a trace. One errant website visit could mean that my unobscured IP address is sitting in a database somewhere waiting like a timebomb to destroy my anonymity in its entirety.

It is also rather difficult to promote this project in an anonymous fashion. My typical first line of promotion for a project like this would be talking it over with friends and family. This way I gain eyeballs and received needed feedback. This time, however, nobody knows that I’m even working on a project. It is quite lonely. One day I will write something worth posting to Hacker News or Reddit and people may get curious and a handful may find their way back to this post. For now these words go unnoticed. They are the cry into a crowded room that receives no echo… or a different, better metaphor that doesn’t sound so emo.
