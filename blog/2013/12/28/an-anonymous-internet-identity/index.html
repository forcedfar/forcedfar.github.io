An Anonymous Internet Identity

Dec 28th, 2013

It turns out that setting up a fully anonymous online identity is more of a hassle than I thought. I expected I would be able to set up a Gmail account through Tor, use that Gmail account to sign up for Twitter and Github, and be self-publishing in no time. Unfortunately, I ran into a few unforeseen problems.

Reputable email providers require phone numbers

It turns out that Gmail, Yahoo! Mail and whatever Microsoft calls Hotmail these days all require phone verification to set up an account. After a bit of searching, I found that Russia-based Yandex was probably my best option. I don’t love that. Because Yandex’s mail service is often associated with scammers and spammers, a yandex.com email address is like a nice big red flag to anyone with whom I communicate. If you have a better suggestion for a trustworthy, free webmail provider that will let me sign up through Tor and won’t require an email address, please send a message to my Github username at yandex dot com.

Twitter has suspended my account

UPDATE: Twitter account now enabled

You can try to follow me on Twitter, but I doubt you’ll get very far. My account has been suspended. I don’t know whether it is due to my creating an account through a Tor node or my yandex email address, but before I had the chance to follow a single soul or tweet a single character, I got a message across the top of my Twitter window:

Your account (@forcedfar) is currently suspended. For more information, please visit Suspended Accounts.

That was fast. Although I did get to skip the page that forces strongly suggests you follow five people before moving on. So I got that going for me. Which is nice. I have submitted a support ticket which I hope will result in access to full use of Twitter. The ticket explained in perfect English how I am not a robot and have no intention of ever being one.

Some things worked just fine

The main bright spot was that Github allowed me full use of their platform with no prejudice. I had to do a little Googling to make sure I could push to them through Tor, but Github was accepting of me despite my shady email and dubious IP address. I guess it makes sense that bad actors would be more interested in exploiting services like Twitter over services like Github. There’s probably not a lot of money in maliciously contributing to open source projects.
